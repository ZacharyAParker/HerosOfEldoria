# World Bible — Aethel

Personal reference so I don't contradict myself mid-session. If something that happens at the
table (or a recap, or something I improvise) conflicts with this doc, this doc wins until I sit
down and deliberately change it.

---

## 1. The World of Aethel

- **World name:** Aethel
- **Known continent:** Aeridor — this is the only continent we actually play on. There are seven
  others out there but I haven't named or mapped any of them, and I'm keeping it that way. If a
  plot ever needs "a stranger from across the sea," that's a sit-down-and-actually-decide moment,
  not something to improvise at the table.
- **Calendar:** Years run in KE (Kingdom Era), counted from Theron the Great's coronation.
  (Old World Anvil notes said "BCE" — that was just a typo on my part, canon is KE.)
- **Current year: 1754 KE** — three years after the old campaign (1751 KE).

## 2. The Kingdom of Eldoria

Prosperous kingdom under the golden stag banner — rich farmland at the center, wilder the further
out you get. Calm and comfortable on the surface; scholars privately call this stretch the Age of
Decay, a gilded, brittle peace. The kingdom is rich, soft, nostalgic, and bored out of its mind.

That boredom is the engine of the new campaign — a kingdom this starved for heroes will believe in
anyone who even looks like one.

### The adventuring fad (1752–present)

Adventuring isn't really a profession in Eldoria right now, it's a fashion trend, and the whole
new campaign (including the villain's whole scheme) grows out of that soil. Worth keeping straight
why it actually happened, in order:

1. **Peace surplus.** No war since 1704, no orc raid in fifty years, the frontier near the
   heartland tamed. Two generations of martial energy and restless money with nowhere to go. The
   Age of Decay didn't kill the appetite for glory, it just took away every legit outlet for it.
2. **Democratized heroism.** Every older Eldorian hero myth starred kings and paladins. The
   ballads about the old party (§4) star seven broke misfits who look like your neighbor, your
   cousin, you. The accidental message every bored apprentice in the kingdom absorbed: anyone can
   do this. Probably the most socially explosive idea Elysia ever wrote, and she did it by
   accident.
3. **Perceived safety.** The public version of the Darkest Hour (§4) says death itself turned the
   worthy away at the door. A whole generation quietly decided mortality is negotiable if your
   heart's pure. The Church of Brantifax preaches against this constantly — "it was a joke, and
   the Door only laughs once" — and it doesn't stick. Doesn't help that most adventuring near
   civilization is D-license pest control, so amateurs mostly survive, which just confirms the
   illusion.
4. **The institutional flywheel.** Questionable Heroes Day (1752) made it a holiday → the
   Licensing Act (1752, §8) made it a credential → credentials became status symbols → status
   symbols grew a whole industry: outfitters, insurers, monster-trophy dealers, the Swiftvale
   Academy, pilgrimage tourism (Barty's fake table, Popip's statue, Saint Scorri's shrine potions,
   Vexthrax's 1 cp entry fee). Adventuring is an economy now, and economies advertise.
5. **Nostalgia politics.** The old martial houses (Duke Valerius loudest of all) miss the Age of
   Iron and read the Scholar King as decline personified. Adventurers-as-spectator-sport lets the
   kingdom feel like the songs again without an actual war. Partly the aristocracy grieving its
   own relevance in costume.

On the ground (free to invent more of this): D-class badges worn as jewelry by nobles who've
never left Silverhaven, outfitters selling boots that have never touched mud, taverns rebranding
as "guildhalls," retired guardsmen hired as chaperones for merchants' sons, a brisk trade in
mostly-fake monster trophies (Grimble Foulwhistle's whole supply chain — see campaign overview),
ballad pub quizzes where every "correct" answer is wrong, and a small, growing pile of amateur
casualty reports the crown doesn't publicize and R.O.M.T. quietly files away.

Why it matters: this fad is the medium the whole conspiracy grows in. Quill didn't invent Eldoria's
hunger for heroes, he's just the first person to monetize it at scale, and Lucian is the product
built exactly for this market. The fad's real casualties — drowned dilettantes, provoked monsters,
uninsured property damage — are the unglamorous caseload of the party's actual job. The kingdom
plays all of it completely straight, which is the joke.

### Other peoples

- **Dwarves of Khaz-Torim** — mountain kingdom to the north, allies and trade partners since the
  Great Concordance (1100 KE).
- **Elves of the Whisperwood** — ancient, insular, used to run humanity (see history below). They
  remember being on top. Humans mostly don't. Awkward.
- **Orc hordes of the north** — beaten fifty years ago in the War of the Broken Spear, quiet since.

## 4. The old campaign — now canon history

"The Abduction of Princess Aurelia" (1751 KE) happened, all of it, the players know this. It's
resolved, closed, and good for easter eggs now — not the plot of the new campaign.

### What actually happened (the true version)

1. Princess Aurelia, the kingdom's sharpest amateur alchemist, was poking around the Whisperwood
   Peaks and got too close to a goblin excavation. The bugbear shaman Mog'Thul (secretly serving a
   nameless dark god) paid a young green dragon, Vexthrax, to "abduct" her as a distraction. The
   "snatched from the royal gardens" story was a palace cover-up for the fact that she'd snuck out
   — so the gardens version was always the official lie.
2. Seven misfits took the 1,000 gp rescue contract: Felisi, Sylven Swiftvale, Clara Elizabeth
   Godwin, Ambrossa D'Onforia, Ollie, Elysia Moonstrum, Popip Bernger (replaced after his death by
   Scorri Veloci).
3. It was a disaster that stumbled into success — a public tavern defecation, a goblin mascot
   (Taylor) sent to her death, a full TPK, a mysterious mass resurrection, a fatal potion overdose
   immediately after that resurrection, a love potion, and Vexthrax getting permanently shrunk to
   chicken size. Aurelia got rescued, and was unimpressed.
4. Aurelia secretly hired them afterward to stop Mog'Thul from unearthing the Eye Beneath the
   Stone.

### How the loose threads resolved (offscreen, 1751)

- **The Eye Beneath the Stone** — an ancient elven ward-anchor, the keystone of a sealing lattice
  the elves buried under the Peaks during the Dominion. Not a weapon, a lock. Mog'Thul's dark god
  wanted it cracked open. The party sabotaged the ritual (how much of that was on purpose is
  debatable), the Eye got recovered intact, hauled to Silverhaven, and sealed in the Royal Vault,
  where it still sits — inert, filed, guarded. Closed thread. If I ever want to reopen the Eye or
  the dark god, that's a deliberate decision I write down here first, not something I improvise.
- **Mog'Thul** — defeated at the ruins. Official record: "troublesome goblins dealt with." His dark
  god never got a name and went quiet. Closed, unless I decide otherwise.
- **The mysterious stranger (the mass resurrection)** — Brantifax, the Laughing Door, god of
  fools, gamblers and second chances (§6), acting through his herald, the wandering bard Elara.
  He brought the whole party back because, per church doctrine, "that ending was too stupid to
  count." Important: this was a one-time divine joke. Brantifax has never done it again and won't
  do it for the new party. Death is real this time.
- **Vexthrax's curse** — permanent. The wild-magic backlash that shrank him can't be undone (a lot
  of expensive wizards have confirmed this, to his fury). More on him in §5.
- **Aurelia's secret agents** — after the Whisperwood Affair the arrangement got quietly
  formalized, then quietly retired once the seven got too famous to stay deniable. Their pension:
  silence, gold, and a national holiday about the wrong version of events.
- **The Red Tusk goblins** — leaderless after Mog'Thul fell, tribe scattered. Most went legit
  (couriers, laborers, one theatrical costumer — see the villain roster in the campaign overview).
- **The "traitor" note / grizzled ranger** — fully vindicated, it really was bird-watching. The
  Oakhaven Ornithological Society is still going (§5).

### The ballad rule (how I get to retcon freely)

Everything the public knows about 1751 comes from Elysia Moonstrum's ballads, and Elysia got
almost everything wrong — partly artistic license, partly because she died mid-adventure.
In-world common knowledge is a heroic, sanitized, factually disastrous version of events. This is
my main tool for the reboot: whenever new canon needs to differ from what the players remember,
the difference is diegetic — the ballads lied. Players who were there know the truth, the world
believes the song. I can change anything about the old campaign on-screen, as a running joke,
without it ever being a plot hole.

### The ballad canon (what the kingdom actually believes)

The public story of 1751 lives in Elysia's song cycle, "The Lay of the Questionable Seven" —
eight ballads performed every Questionable Heroes Day, taught to kids, quoted by drunks, the
answer key to every pub quiz in Eldoria. Ballad titles (flexible, just flavor): The Snatching of
the Rose · Seven Answered · The Night of the Gilded Flagon · Taylor the True · The Darkest Hour ·
The Second Swig · The Greenfall · The Rose Returned.

Important nuance: the ballads don't paint the Seven as flawless — that would never have sold in
the Age of Decay, and Elysia knew her audience. The trick is that the songs keep the outline of
the chaos and sand every failure into charm. The Seven of the songs are lovable underdogs whose
every screwup turns out, by the last verse, to have been destiny in disguise. That's what
"Questionable Heroes" means to the public — not "dubious," but "unlikely, and that's the miracle."
That framing is exactly what made the story catch on so hard (see the fad, §2).

| # | What the ballads say | What actually happened |
|---|---|---|

| 5 | The Darkest Hour: the Seven fell in battle, and Death itself, moved by their valor, turned them back at the door. | A full TPK, reversed by Brantifax because the ending was "too stupid to count." ### Why the lie stuck

This bugged me if I didn't write it down: why doesn't anyone figure out the ballads are wrong?
Five reasons, and each one's a lever if a player ever pushes on it:

1. **The only chronicler was dead for the important parts.** Elysia died mid-adventure and got
   resurrected near the end, so big chunks of her own source material happened while she was a
   corpse. She rebuilt it from hearsay, filled the gaps with craft, and graded herself on a curve.
   She's never denied it — ask her and she'll say, cheerfully, "I was dead for that bit, I made it
   better."
2. **The crown endorsed it.** The gardens verse protects Aurelia's reputation, the missing
   Whisperwood Affair protects a state secret, a heroic myth flatters a nervous monarchy. Elysia's
   Court Bard job is quietly an editorial position — this is the official history, with a royal
   seal on it.
3. **The witnesses got paid.** The Seven's pension was literally silence and gold. Everyone who
   could correct the record is comfortably invested in not doing so. (And the one who talks the
   most — Vexthrax — has zero credibility.)
4. **The kingdom wanted it to be true.** The Age of Decay was starving for exactly this story.
   Nobody fact-checks the thing that finally makes them feel alive, they buy the merch instead.
   Demand pulled the record toward the lie.
5. **The truth is unbelievable.** The real events — the defecation, the trap-check mascot, the
   overdose right after the resurrection — sound like slander against national heroes. Anyone who
   tells the truth about 1751 sounds like a crank, and a small furious drake in Oakhaven has spent
   three years proving that.

If I use the "ballads lied" gag at the table, worth naming which lock is holding that particular
lie in place — keeps it consistent, and locks 1 and 3 can actually be picked (Elysia confronted
with evidence, a pensioned hero tempted to talk). Lock 2 only opens if Aurelia opens it. Locks 4
and 5 never open — the public will believe the ballads forever, which is exactly why this new
party will never be famous no matter what they pull off. That's the point.

## 5. Where the old cast is now

Cameo-ready epilogues, flexible on detail, but the fates themselves are set. If anyone wants their
old PC back, I need to upgrade them from cameo to full NPC (or retire them properly) before
session 1 and update this doc.

- **Felisi** — took the money and vanished. Rumor has it the masked proprietor "Madame Whiskers"
  of a Silverhaven gambling den is charismatic, feline-eyed, always paid in advance. Unconfirmed.
- **Sylven Swiftvale** — founded the Swiftvale Academy for Unconventional Companions, Eldoria's
  first monster-taming finishing school. The brochure features a tiny green drake who no longer
  attends and demands royalties.
- **Clara Elizabeth Godwin** — actually enlisted this time. Now Ser Clara of the Royal Guard, drill
  instructor, secretly still terrified of combat, decorated twice. The lie became true and nobody
  noticed it used to be a lie.
- **Ollie** — retired executioner, runs The Chopping Block, an axe-throwing tavern in Oakhaven.
  Sign on the door: "NO ELVES." Smaller, newer, below it: "fine. some elves."
- **Ambrossa D'Onforia** — reclaimed her noble name. House D'Onforia now offers "discreet
  acquisitions and estate solutions." Nobody asks.
- **Elysia Moonstrum** — the love potion faded into a mortifying friendship with the princess. Now
  Court Bard of Silverhaven, author of the wildly inaccurate ballads (§4).
- **Scorri Veloci** — died a third time at his own retirement party (unattended potion). Church of
  Brantifax canonized him: Saint Scorri of the Second Swig, patron of bad decisions. Roadside
  shrines hold a rack of unlabeled potions. Devout travelers drink one.
- **Popip Bernger** — memorial statue in Oakhaven's square, plaque reads "BEWARE FALLING ROCKS." A
  small boulder is chained respectfully next to it.
- **Vexthrax, the Verdant Scourge** — still chicken-sized, still furious about it. Runs "THE HOARD"
  in Oakhaven, part pawnshop, part museum of his own former greatness, 1 cp entry fee ("a
  tribute"). Smallest dragon hoard on record, guarded by the biggest ego around. Kleptomania:
  mostly managed. Great recurring NPC — knows everyone, hears everything, trades info for shiny
  objects and flattery. Per the ballad canon the public thinks Vexthrax died at the Greenfall, so
  everyone assumes THE HOARD's owner is just impersonating a dead dragon. He's the actual truth of
  1751, alive, screaming, and completely disbelieved.
- **Taylor (the goblin)** — dead, but remembered. Ex-Red Tusk couriers now run as "Taylor's
  Runners," fastest post in the Reaches, badge is a small red heart.
- **The grizzled ranger** — vindicated, now honorary president of the Oakhaven Ornithological
  Society, which knows what's moving in the wilds days before the crown does. Useful contact.
- **Barty Stoutkeg** — still pours at the Gilded Flagon, still gossips, charges tourists to see
  "the actual table" from the ballads. It is not the actual table.
- **Princess Aurelia (22)** — full entry, §7.
- **Ser Kaelan** — full entry, §7. His reward for competence was a promotion.

## 7. Key living NPCs

### King Theron IV, the Scholar King (50s, alive)
Diplomat, historian, allergic to war and increasingly to surprises. The martial old houses read
his thoughtfulness as weakness. Became protective of Aurelia to a smothering degree after 1751.
Voice: measured, kindly, sighs like a library draft.

### Crown Princess Aurelia (22, alive)
Brilliant, sarcastic, patient the way a chess player is patient. The palace won't let her do
anything, so she built something instead — a tiny, deniable office for problems the Royal Guard is
too slow, too visible, or too dignified to touch (R.O.M.T., §8). Learned one lesson from 1751:
never rely on famous heroes, hire nobodies. Voice: dry, rapid, never raises it.

### Ser Kaelan, Knight-Commander (40s, alive)
Promoted for surviving the last batch of heroes. Runs R.O.M.T. day-to-day, a job he describes as
"herding weaponized toddlers." Grim, incorruptible, keeps a drawer of resignation letters he never
sends. Voice: flat, clipped, weaponized patience.

### Ser Clara of the Royal Guard (mid-20s)
See §5. If her old player doesn't reclaim her, she's the new party's sympathetic inside contact —
the only Guard officer who remembers what real chaos looks like and covers for the party
accordingly.

### Duke Valerius of the Western Marches (60s)
Old lion of the martial faction, loudly nostalgic for the Age of Iron, loudly disappointed in the
Scholar King. Not a villain, but exactly the kind of proud, frustrated audience a fake hero was
built to seduce. Useful dupe, possible late ally.

## 8. Institutions of the new era

### R.O.M.T. — the Royal Office of Miscellaneous Threats (the party's employer)
Founded quietly by Aurelia in 1753. Budget: embarrassing. Office: a converted stable behind the
Silverhaven mews — yes, the stable from the ballads, the lantern's still there, nobody but Aurelia
knows it matters. Mandate: investigate and resolve threats too weird, too small, or too
politically awkward for the Royal Guard. Staff: Ser Kaelan (director, unwilling), one clerk
(Miriam, elderly, unshockable, secretly the best-informed person in the capital), and now the
player characters. Unofficial motto: "Somebody has to."

### The Adventurer's Licensing Act of 1752
After Questionable Heroes Day made adventuring fashionable (§2), the crown started licensing
adventurers to stem the flood of amateurs. Classes D through S, annual renewal, property-damage
insurance riders. No S-class license has ever been issued, though two dukes have applied. Good
bureaucratic comedy: the party, as crown agents, technically outrank licensed adventurers, and are
technically unlicensed themselves.

### The Oakhaven Ornithological Society
Bird-watchers. Also, accidentally, the best intelligence network in eastern Eldoria. They'll trade
info for rare-bird sightings, but their reports have to be decoded out of birding jargon first.
