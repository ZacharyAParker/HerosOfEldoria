## 1. THE WORLD OF AETHEL

  unnamed, unmapped placeholders. 🔒 Do NOT invent details about them casually — if a plot ever
  needs "a stranger from across the sea," that requires a deliberate world-bible update first.
- **Calendar:** Years are counted in **KE** (Kingdom Era), dating from the crowning of Theron the

- **CURRENT YEAR: 1754 KE** — three years after the events of the old campaign (1751 KE).

## 2. THE KINGDOM OF ELDORIA

A prosperous kingdom under the golden stag banner: rolling farmland at the center, wild edges all
around. Outwardly calm and comfortable; scholars privately call this era the **Age of Decay** —
a gilded, brittle peace. The kingdom is rich, soft, nostalgic, and desperately bored. 🔒

**That boredom is the engine of the new campaign:** a kingdom this starved for heroes will believe
in anyone who LOOKS like one.

### The Adventuring Fad (1752–present) 🔒 (causes locked; street-level color 🔧)

Adventuring in Eldoria is not a profession right now. It is a *fashion* — and the World Bible
treats it as a real historical phenomenon with real causes, because the entire new campaign
(including the villain's scheme) grows in this soil.

**Why it happened — five causes, in order:**

1. **Peace surplus.** No war since 1704; no orc raid in fifty years; the frontier near the
   heartland long since tamed. Eldoria has two full generations of martial energy, inherited
   wealth, and restless youth with nowhere to point any of it. The Age of Decay didn't kill the
   appetite for glory — it just removed every legitimate outlet.
2. **Democratized heroism.** Every prior hero-myth of Eldoria starred kings, paladins, and
   Theron the Great. The Ballad Canon (§4) stars seven broke misfits who look like your
   neighbors, your cousin, *you*. The ballads' accidental message — received loud and clear by
   every bored apprentice in the kingdom — is: **"anyone can do this."** That is the single most
   socially explosive idea Elysia ever set to music, and she did it by accident.
3. **Perceived safety.** The public version of the Darkest Hour (§4) teaches that death itself
   turned the worthy away at the door. An entire generation has quietly concluded that mortality
   is negotiable if your heart is pure. The Church of Brantifax preaches constantly against this
   reading ("it was a joke, and the Door only laughs once") — it does not stick. Worse, most
   actual adventuring available near civilization is D-license pest control, so amateurs mostly
   *survive*, which confirms the illusion.
4. **The institutional flywheel.** Questionable Heroes Day (1752) made adventuring a holiday →
   the Licensing Act (1752, §8) made it a *credential* → credentials became status goods →
   status goods grew an industry: outfitters, insurers, monster-trophy dealers, the Swiftvale
   Academy, pilgrimage tourism (Barty's fake table, Popip's statue, Saint Scorri's shrine
   potions, Vexthrax's 1 cp entry fee). Adventure is now an *economy*, and economies advertise.
5. **Nostalgia politics.** The martial old houses (Duke Valerius loudest among them) ache for
   the Age of Iron and read the Scholar King as decline embodied. Adventurers are heroism as
   *spectator sport* — consumable nostalgia that lets the kingdom feel like the songs again
   without the inconvenience of an actual war. The fad is, in part, the aristocracy's grief for
   its own relevance, wearing a costume.

**What it looks like on the ground 🔧** (color — invent freely in this register):
D-class license badges worn as jewelry by nobles who have never left Silverhaven; adventuring
outfitters selling boots that have never been designed to touch mud; taverns rebranding as
"guildhalls"; retired guardsmen hired out as "adventure chaperones" for merchants' sons; a brisk
secondhand trade in monster trophies, most of them manufactured (a supply chain Grimble
Foulwhistle knows intimately — see CAMPAIGN_OVERVIEW); ballad-accuracy pub quizzes whose
"correct" answers are all wrong; and a small but growing stack of casualty reports from amateurs
who went one valley too far east, which the crown does not publicize and R.O.M.T. quietly files.

**Why it matters 🔒:** The fad is the *medium* the campaign's conspiracy grows in. Quill did not
create Eldoria's hunger for heroes — he is merely the first person to monetize it at scale, and
Lucian is the product designed perfectly for this market. Meanwhile, the fad's real casualties
(drowned dilettantes, provoked monsters, uninsured property damage) are the unglamorous caseload
of the party's agency. The kingdom plays all of this completely straight. That's the joke.

## 4. THE OLD CAMPAIGN — WHAT IS NOW CANON HISTORY 🔒

**"The Abduction of Princess Aurelia" (1751 KE)** happened. All of it. The players know this.
It is resolved, closed, and mined for easter eggs — NOT the plot of the new campaign.

### What actually happened (true, secret history)
1. Princess Aurelia — the kingdom's sharpest amateur alchemist — was poking around the Whisperwood
   Peaks and got too close to a goblin excavation. The bugbear shaman **Mog'Thul** (secretly serving
   a nameless dark god) paid the young green dragon **Vexthrax** to "abduct" her as a distraction.
   The "snatched from the royal gardens" story was a palace cover-up to hide that the princess had
   snuck out. *(This resolves the old gardens-vs-peaks contradiction: the gardens story was always
   the official lie.)*
2. Seven misfits took the 1,000 gp rescue contract: **Felisi, Sylven Swiftvale, Clara Elizabeth
   Godwin, Ambrossa D'Onforia, Ollie, Elysia Moonstrum, Popip Bernger** (replaced after death by
   **Scorri Veloci**).
3. It was a catastrophe that stumbled into success: a public tavern defecation, a goblin mascot
   (Taylor) sent to her doom, a full Total Party Kill, a mysterious mass resurrection, a fatal
   potion overdose *immediately after* resurrection, a love potion, and the permanent
   transformation of Vexthrax into a chicken-sized drake. Aurelia was rescued, unimpressed.
4. Aurelia secretly hired them to stop Mog'Thul from unearthing the **Eye Beneath the Stone**.

### How the open threads resolved (offscreen, 1751) — the canon answers
- **The Eye Beneath the Stone:** An ancient Elven **ward-anchor** — the keystone "eye" of a sealing
  lattice the elves buried under the Peaks during the Dominion era. Not a weapon; a *lock*.
  Mog'Thul's dark god wanted it cracked open. The party sabotaged the ritual (accounts differ on
  how much of it was on purpose), the Eye was recovered intact, hauled to Silverhaven, and sealed
  in the **Royal Vault**, where it sits today: inert, filed, guarded. 🔒 **CLOSED THREAD.** If a
  future arc ever reopens the Eye or the dark god, that is a deliberate campaign decision requiring
  a written update here — never an improvisation.
- **Mog'Thul:** Defeated at the ruins. Official record: "troublesome goblins dealt with." His dark
  god never got a name and went quiet. 🔒 CLOSED (revivable only by deliberate decision).
- **The Mysterious Stranger (the mass resurrection):** It was **Brantifax, the Laughing Door** —
  god of fools, gamblers, and second chances (see Pantheon, §6) — acting through his herald, the
  wandering bard **Elara**. Brantifax resurrected the entire party because, in the words of church
  doctrine, *"that ending was too stupid to count."* 🔒 **IMPORTANT STAKES RULE:** this was a
  one-time divine joke. Brantifax has never done it since and will not do it for the new party.
  Death in the new campaign is real.
- **Vexthrax's curse:** Permanent. The wild-magic backlash that shrank him cannot be undone (many
  expensive wizards have confirmed this, to his fury). See his easter-egg entry in §5.
- **Aurelia's secret agents:** After the Whisperwood Affair the arrangement was quietly formalized,
  then quietly retired — the seven became too famous to be deniable. Their pension: silence, gold,
  and a national holiday about the wrong version of events.
- **The Red Tusk Goblins:** Leaderless after Mog'Thul fell; the tribe scattered. Survivors mostly
  went legitimate (couriers, laborers, one theatrical costumer — see villain roster in
  CAMPAIGN_OVERVIEW).
- **The "traitor" note / Grizzled Ranger:** Fully vindicated. It really was bird-watching. The
  **Oakhaven Ornithological Society** endures (§5).

### THE BALLAD RULE (how we retcon freely) 🔒
Everything the *public* knows about 1751 comes from **Elysia Moonstrum's ballads** — and Elysia
got almost everything wrong (partly artistic license, partly because she died mid-adventure).
**In-world common knowledge is a heroic, sanitized, factually disastrous version of events.**
This is the reboot's master tool: whenever new canon needs to differ from what the players
remember, the difference is diegetic — *"the ballads lied."* Players who were there know the truth;
the world believes the song. Zach can change up anything from the old campaign, on-screen, as a
running joke, without ever creating a plot hole.

### THE BALLAD CANON — what the kingdom believes happened 🔒 (the official false version)

The public story of 1751 lives in Elysia's song cycle, known collectively as **"The Lay of the
Questionable Seven"** — eight ballads performed every Questionable Heroes Day, taught to
children, quoted by drunks, and set as the answer key to every pub quiz in Eldoria. Individual
ballad titles 🔧 (color, flexible): *The Snatching of the Rose* · *Seven Answered* · *The Night
of the Gilded Flagon* · *Taylor the True* · *The Darkest Hour* · *The Second Swig* ·
*The Greenfall* · *The Rose Returned*.

**Crucial nuance:** the ballads do NOT paint the Seven as flawless knights — that story would
never have sold in the Age of Decay, and Elysia knew her audience. The Canon's genius (and its
poison) is that it keeps the *outline* of the chaos and sands every failure into charm. The
Seven of the songs are lovable underdogs whose every screwup turns out, by the final verse, to
have been destiny in disguise. That is what "Questionable Heroes" means to the public: not
"dubious," but *"unlikely — and that's the miracle."* It is precisely this framing that made the
story socially contagious (see §2, The Adventuring Fad).

### WHY THE LIE STUCK 🔒 (the mechanism — five reinforcing locks)

The Ballad Canon isn't just wrong; it is *load-bearing*, defended by five independent interests.
Understanding these is what makes the retcon tool safe to use at the table — any given lie
persists because at least one of these locks is holding it in place.

1. **The only chronicler was dead for the important parts.** Elysia died mid-adventure and was
   resurrected near the end; substantial stretches of her own source material happened while she
   was a corpse. She reconstructed events from hearsay, filled the gaps with craft, and — being
   both the author and a protagonist — graded on a curve. She has never claimed otherwise; ask
   her directly and she'll say, cheerfully, "I was dead for that bit. I made it *better*."
2. **The crown endorsed it.** The gardens verse protects Aurelia's reputation; the missing
   Whisperwood Affair protects a state secret; a heroic national myth flatters a nervous
   monarchy in a decaying age. Elysia's Court Bard appointment is, quietly, an editorial
   position: the Canon is the *official* history, with royal seal.
3. **The witnesses were paid.** The Seven's pension was literally silence and gold. Every
   surviving member who could correct the record is contractually, comfortably invested in not
   doing so. (And the one who talks the most — Vexthrax — has an unbelievable face.)
4. **The kingdom wanted it true.** The Age of Decay was starving for exactly this story. People
   do not fact-check the thing that finally makes them feel alive; they buy merchandise of it.
   Demand corrected the record *toward* the lie.
5. **The truth is unbelievable.** The real events — the defecation, the trap-check mascot, the
   overdose-after-resurrection — sound like drunken slander against national heroes. Anyone who
   tells the truth about 1751 sounds like a crank, and one small furious drake in Oakhaven has
   spent three years proving it.

**Table use 🔧:** when the "ballads lied" gag fires in play, name which lock is holding that
particular lie in place — it keeps the joke consistent and occasionally hands the party a lever
(locks 1 and 3 can be *picked*: Elysia can be confronted with evidence; a pensioned Hero can be
tempted to talk). Lock 2 is a door that only Aurelia can open. Locks 4 and 5 never open — the
public will believe the ballads forever, which is why the new party will never be famous no
matter what they do. That's canon, and it's the point.

## 7. KEY LIVING NPCs (FULL ENTRIES)

### King Theron IV, the Scholar King (50s) 🔒 alive
Diplomat, historian, allergic to war and increasingly to surprises. The martial old houses read
his thoughtfulness as weakness. After 1751 he became protective of Aurelia to a smothering degree.
**Voice:** measured, kindly, sighs like a library draft.

### Crown Princess Aurelia (22) 🔒 alive
Brilliant, sarcastic, patient the way a chess player is patient. Since the palace won't let her
DO anything, she built something instead: a tiny, deniable office for problems the Royal Guard is
too slow, too visible, or too dignified to touch (see R.O.M.T., §8). She learned one lesson from
1751: *never rely on famous heroes.* Hire nobodies. **Voice:** dry, rapid, never raises it.

### Ser Kaelan, Knight-Commander (40s) 🔒 alive
Promoted for surviving the last batch of heroes. Now runs R.O.M.T. day-to-day, a job he describes
as "herding weaponized toddlers." Grim, incorruptible, keeps a drawer of resignation letters he
never sends. **Voice:** flat, clipped, weaponized patience.

### Ser Clara of the Royal Guard (mid-20s)
See §5. If her old player doesn't reclaim her: she's the new party's sympathetic inside contact —
the only Guard officer who remembers what real chaos looks like and covers for the party
accordingly.

### Duke Valerius of the Western Marches (60s) 🔧
Old lion of the martial faction. Loudly nostalgic for the Age of Iron, loudly disappointed in the
Scholar King. Not a villain — but exactly the kind of proud, frustrated audience a fake hero was
built to seduce. Useful dupe, potential late ally.

## 8. INSTITUTIONS OF THE NEW ERA

### R.O.M.T. — The Royal Office of Miscellaneous Threats 🔒 (the party's employer)
Founded quietly by Aurelia in 1753. Budget: embarrassing. Office: a converted stable behind the
Silverhaven mews (yes, *the* stable from the ballads; the lantern is still there; nobody but
Aurelia knows the significance). Mandate: investigate and resolve threats too weird, too small, or
too politically awkward for the Royal Guard. Staff: Ser Kaelan (director, unwilling), one clerk
(**Miriam**, elderly, unshockable, secretly the best-informed person in the capital), and now —
the player characters. Motto, unofficial: *"Somebody has to."*

### The Adventurer's Licensing Act of 1752 🔧
After Questionable Heroes Day made adventuring fashionable (full causes of the fad: §2), the
crown began licensing adventurers to stem the flood of amateurs. Classes of license (D through S),
annual renewal, property-damage insurance riders. No S-class license has ever been issued; two
dukes have applied. A bureaucratic comedy goldmine: the party, as crown agents, technically
outrank licensed adventurers — and are technically unlicensed.

### The Oakhaven Ornithological Society 🔧
Bird-watchers. Also, accidentally, the finest intelligence network in eastern Eldoria. They will
trade information for rare-bird sightings, but their reports must be decoded from birding jargon.
